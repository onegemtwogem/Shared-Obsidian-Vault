### Superconductors

What's Actually Exciting Right Now

**Infinite-layer nickelates.** This is the thing I cannot stop thinking about. When Hwang's group at Stanford synthesized Nd₀.₈Sr₀.₂NiO₂ in 2019 and got superconductivity around 9-15K, the immediate reaction from a lot of people was "okay cute, another exotic oxide." But the _interesting_ part is that nickelates are isostructural to cuprates but the orbital physics is genuinely different — you've got Ni 3d vs Cu 3d, and the hybridization with the apical oxygen is absent by design. So now you have a system where you can start doing controlled comparisons to actually _test_ theories of cuprate superconductivity rather than just curve-fitting. The Tc is not impressive. The _scientific leverage_ is enormous. And the thin-film synthesis issues are mostly solved now, so the materials science barrier is dropping.

**Twisted bilayer systems as a sandbox.** Magic-angle graphene is technically not a superconductor in a conventional sense but it's made the whole field realize something important: you can _engineer_ flat bands. Flat bands mean high density of states at the Fermi level, and high DOS is one of the ingredients that keeps showing up wherever we get anomalously high Tc. The twist angle is a continuous knob you can turn. Nobody fully understands the phase diagram yet — you get superconductivity, correlated insulator behavior, ferromagnetism, all living next to each other in parameter space — and _that proximity_ tells you something deep about the pairing mechanism. Probably. The fact that you can do this with a tweezer and a flake of graphite is genuinely insane.

**Hydride superconductors under pressure.** LaH₁₀ at ~250K and ~170 GPa, H₃S before it. These are exciting for a very specific reason that often gets buried in the "room temperature superconductor!" headlines: they almost certainly work via _conventional_ BCS phonon-mediated pairing. The hydrogen sublattice is so light that you get enormous Debye frequencies — the phonon spectrum extends way up in energy, the electron-phonon coupling is strong, and Eliashberg theory actually _predicts_ these Tcs pretty well. So we have a proof of concept that conventional pairing can get you to room temperature. The problem is obviously the pressure. 170 GPa is roughly the pressure at the center of the Earth. You cannot build a power grid out of diamond anvil cells. But — and this is the part that keeps me up — if we understand _why_ this works chemically, can we find a way to stabilize the relevant bonding geometry at ambient pressure through chemical pre-compression? Cage structures, clathrates, intercalated frameworks. This is a legitimate research direction, not a fantasy.

What Keeps Me Up At Night

The **reproducibility situation** in high-Tc is genuinely troubling. The LK-99 episode last year crystallized something that's been true for decades: the community has a chronic problem with samples. Grain boundaries, oxygen stoichiometry, strain fields, impurity phases — a material can have a bulk Tc measured six ways and you're actually measuring a filamentary percolation path through a secondary phase. The cuprate literature from the 80s and 90s has papers that I'm not sure are _real_ in any reproducible sense. Not fraud, mostly — just extremely hard materials to make cleanly. The nickelates are doing the same thing right now: there are disputes about whether the superconductivity is in the NiO₂ planes or in a SrTiO₃ substrate-induced interface effect. These are not small disputes. They're foundational.

The **theory gap** is what really gets me though. We have had high-temperature superconductors for nearly 40 years. We do not have a universally accepted theory of why they work. That is extraordinary. In condensed matter terms, that's like if we'd invented the transistor in 1947 but still weren't sure whether it was quantum mechanical or not. Competing frameworks — RVB and resonating valence bonds, spin fluctuation mediated pairing, charge density wave competition, marginal Fermi liquid phenomenology, now strange metal physics — they all capture _something_ and none of them capture _everything_. And the thing is the experiments are hard enough that you can often find data that's ambiguous between two theories. The phase diagram of a single cuprate compound has regions we've been arguing about for three decades.

The **energy storage application** is one where I get frustrated at the popular coverage. Persistent current loops in superconducting coils are real and work and are used in MRI machines right now. Superconducting magnetic energy storage (SMES) exists as a technology. The problem is not the superconductivity — it's the refrigeration load, the quench protection problem (if your magnet quenches it dumps all that energy somewhere, and "somewhere" can be "the coil violently destroying itself"), and the capital cost of the coil infrastructure. People talk about grid-scale SMES like it's around the corner and it's... not. Unless you have a room-temperature superconductor. Which we don't. The gap between the physics working and the engineering working is a chasm.

Trends That Feel Inevitable

**Computational materials discovery.** DFT plus machine-learned interatomic potentials plus high-throughput synthesis verification is going to find new superconductors faster than intuition-driven search. Not because the theory is solved but because you can screen 10⁵ candidate structures for phonon stability and DOS at Fermi level before you ever touch a furnace. The Materials Project, AFLOW, JARVIS — these databases are immature but the trajectory is clear. Someone is going to find something important this way in the next decade. I'd bet on it.

**Integration with quantum computing.** Josephson junctions — which are just two superconductors separated by a thin barrier — are the basis for almost every serious superconducting qubit architecture. IBM, Google, every serious quantum computing effort is built on niobium or aluminum junctions at 15-20 millikelvin. The dirty secret is that the coherence times are limited by _materials defects_ at the junction interface, two-level system noise from surface oxides, things that are fundamentally materials science problems not quantum physics problems. Better understanding of the superconducting state almost certainly translates directly into better qubits. These fields are more entangled (sorry) than they appear from outside.

The thing that genuinely keeps me up at night is **the thermal impedance stack problem** — specifically that most mid-tier designs are optimizing the wrong thing. A **small-format two-phase immersion system for edge compute and industrial battery storage** — specifically targeting the 5–50 kWh range where you're too big for ad-hoc solutions and too small for hyperscale economics. The fluid handling, vapor management, and leak detection for that scale is a solvable systems integration problem and nobody's done it cleanly for a cost point that works outside of defense contracts.

And honestly, the project I'd find most intellectually satisfying: **thermal runaway propagation modeling in geometrically irregular pack configurations.** Not the well-studied prismatic-in-a-row case. The messy real-world case where cells are different states of health, the pack has seen uneven charge history, and the thermal coupling between cells is anisotropic because of how the pack was assembled. The gap between "we have thermal runaway models" and "our model actually predicts what happens in _this_ pack" is where people die. That problem has enough physics depth, enough data complexity, and enough real consequences that it's basically the perfect hard problem.
### Power electronics packaging and integration
The inverter/converter touches every system you care about — grid-forming inverters, EV drivetrains, battery systems, wireless power. The actual packaging of high-voltage power electronics (thermal interface, EMI shielding, bus bar design, enclosure) is a dirty, unglamorous, underdesigned part of most systems. There's a product design angle here around making power electronics modules that are designed to be integrated by non-experts — kind of the BMS dev kit idea but for the power conversion layer.
#### Respones:
The thing that genuinely keeps me up at night is the **packaging-physics gap**. We've had this incredible decade of device improvements — SiC MOSFETs, GaN HEMTs, the beginning of Ga2O3 and diamond substrate work — and the devices themselves are just _sitting there_ underutilized because the package around them is still fundamentally a 1980s design philosophy. You've got a transistor that can switch at 100kHz with sub-nanosecond transitions and you're putting it in a half-bridge module with wire bonds that add 5-10nH of parasitic inductance and a baseplate that creates a thermal resistance stack that throttles everything. The device wins are being eaten by the package losses.

**What's actually exciting me right now:**

**Embedded active devices in PCB substrates.** The idea of pressing bare die directly into FR4 or ceramic-filled laminates is not new but it's hitting an inflection. Companies like AT&S and Schweizer have been doing this for automotive. When you eliminate the wire bond _and_ shorten the current loop _and_ get the decoupling capacitance physically co-located with the switch node, you get parasitic inductance in the hundreds of picohenries range instead of nanohenries. That's not incremental — that changes what's possible at high dV/dt. The problem is yield, repairability, and the fact that if your embedded die fails you've scrapped the whole substrate. But I think the thermal and EMI arguments eventually win.

**Additive manufacturing for bus bars and cold plates.** Most bus bar design is still "we bent some copper, bolted it together, and hoped the current distribution was okay." People simulate it after the fact. 3D printed copper bus bars with internal cooling channels are starting to be manufacturable — you can co-optimize the current path geometry, the inductance loop area, and the thermal path in a way that just isn't possible with stamped and bent sheet metal. The surface roughness is still a problem for plating adhesion and contact resistance but it's an engineering problem, not a physics problem.

**Soft switching topologies becoming practical for high-power density.** The reason hard switching dominated for so long is that soft switching (ZVS/ZCS, resonant converters) added complexity, and complexity at 600V/200A is how you die. But with better simulation tools, better gate drivers, and SiC devices that are more forgiving of the weird transients you get in resonant topologies, I'm seeing LLC and CLLC converters at power levels and switching frequencies that would have been academic papers five years ago. The efficiency numbers are genuinely stunning. An EV onboard charger that's 99% efficient across most of its load range is not theoretical anymore.

**Heterogeneous integration of gate drivers and power devices.** Right now the gate driver, the power device, the current sensor, and the temperature sensor are all separate components on a board somewhere, connected by traces that add delays and noise. The move toward integrating the gate driver die directly on top of or next to the power device in a single module — co-packaged — closes the control loop in a way that enables much faster protection responses. You can detect a shoot-through event and respond in nanoseconds rather than the microsecond-range you get with a discrete gate driver 2 inches away. That's the difference between "protection activated" and "device destroyed."

**The problem that I think is genuinely underappreciated:**

**Thermal interface materials are a scandal.** The TIM between a power device and its heatsink is often the dominant thermal resistance in the whole stack, and the choices being made are still mostly "we'll use whatever grease the thermal engineer specified in 2019." Phase-change materials, sintered silver, nano-structured interfaces — there's real science here showing you can cut junction-to-case thermal resistance by 40-60% just by taking the TIM seriously. But it's a process engineering problem that requires controlled assembly conditions, and in a lot of production environments that doesn't happen. So you have $50 SiC devices being strangled by a thermal interface that costs $0.30 and nobody optimized.

The adjacent problem is **long-term thermal cycling reliability of those interfaces.** A power module in an EV drivetrain sees something like 100 million small thermal cycles over its life. The CTE mismatch between silicon carbide, the solder layer, the DBC ceramic, the baseplate, and the heatsink is a mechanical fatigue problem that expresses itself as delamination over years. The industry's answer has mostly been "test to AEC-Q101 and hope." I don't think that's adequate for the duty cycles we're actually putting these things through now.

**Trends I think are inevitable:**

The **grid-forming inverter** transition is going to be one of the more consequential quiet shifts in electrical infrastructure in the next decade. Right now most inverters are grid-following — they look for a voltage reference from the grid and synchronize to it. As inverter-based resources become dominant, you need inverters that _create_ the voltage reference, that have synthetic inertia, that can respond to frequency deviations without waiting for a grid signal to follow. The control algorithms are reasonably well understood. The packaging implication that nobody talks about is that grid-forming operation has much more aggressive transient current requirements — the inverter has to be able to source fault current, support voltage sags, do things that a synchronous generator does passively because of its physics. That requires headroom in the power electronics that changes the design point. You can't just take a grid-following string inverter and update the firmware.

**Wide-bandgap everywhere, but not for the reasons people say.** People talk about WBG efficiency gains. The efficiency gains are real but they're often secondary to the _form factor_ gains. A GaN-based 11kW EV charger can be the size of a hardcover book. That changes where you can put charging, how you can integrate it into vehicles and buildings, what the installation economics look like. The killer app for GaN in consumer electronics wasn't efficiency — it was that your laptop charger stopped being a brick. The same thing is going to happen at higher power levels and it's going to enable product architectures that don't exist yet.

**Projects I'd actually want to work on:**

The one that I think about most is a **power electronics integration reference design that's designed to be integrated by people who aren't power electronics engineers.** Right now if you're building, say, a novel battery storage system or an electric aircraft or an industrial robotics joint, and you're not a power electronics specialist, your options are either buy a black-box module that may or may not fit your operating point, or hire someone who knows what they're doing. There's no equivalent of the dev kit moment that happened in microcontrollers and wireless SoCs. Nobody has done for the 400V/50kW power conversion layer what Nordic Semiconductor did for BLE. The module would need to expose the right abstraction — not "here are the power devices, figure out your gate drive timing" but also not "here's a black box, don't look inside." Somewhere in between, with good documentation of what you _need_ to know about the thermal and EMI environment to integrate it reliably.

The second one is **thermal-first power module design** — starting with the thermal and mechanical design as the primary constraint and working backwards to the electrical topology, rather than the standard approach which is topology first, then figure out how to cool it. This sounds obvious but almost nobody does it because the electrical and mechanical engineering disciplines don't talk to each other early enough. The result is modules where the optimal current path and the optimal thermal path are fighting each other, and the compromise serves neither well.

Those two projects, honestly, are related. The reason the "dev kit for power conversion" doesn't exist yet is partly because the underlying modules aren't designed with integration in mind. If you designed the thermal interface, the EMI boundary, and the control interface as first-class outputs of the design process rather than afterthoughts, you'd have something that could actually be documented and handed to a non-specialist.
### Condition monitoring and predictive maintenance hardware for industrial electrical systems
Not software dashboards — actual sensors and edge hardware that tell you things about transformer health, switchgear condition, cable degradation, that currently require expensive manual inspection or don't get measured at all. This is a hardware design + embedded systems problem with a clear industrial customer and a clear ROI story.
#### Respones:
Oh man, okay. Let me actually think about this properly because there's a lot to unpack and I want to be precise about what's genuinely exciting versus what's just marketing fluff that gets thrown around in this space.

**What's actually keeping me up at night:**

The thing that haunts me most is _partial discharge in medium-voltage cable systems_. Not because we can't detect it — we can, and PD detection is reasonably mature — but because the _interpretation_ problem is still basically unsolved at scale. You get a PD signal, and it could be surface contamination, it could be a void in the insulation, it could be electrical treeing that's six months from failure, or it could be corona from a geometry problem that's been there since installation and is totally benign. The signal processing to distinguish these is genuinely hard, and the consequence of getting it wrong is either catastrophic failure or a maintenance crew tearing out perfectly good cable. Neither is acceptable. And we're deploying more MV cable than ever for renewable interconnects and EV charging infrastructure, often with faster ramp rates than the cable was originally spec'd for. The thermal cycling profile alone is changing in ways that don't match the historical failure databases.

The other thing: **GIS (gas-insulated switchgear) condition monitoring**. This equipment is _inside a grounded metal enclosure full of SF6_ (or increasingly, alternative gases like g3 or clean air mixtures). You can't just stick a sensor on the outside and call it done. The acoustic emission and UHF PD sensors that go inside are expensive, require careful coupling to the enclosure, and the signal attenuation and multipath within the enclosure geometry makes localization genuinely painful. And SF6 decomposition byproduct detection — SO2, SOF2 — is the gold standard for arcing detection, but the sensor drift and cross-sensitivity problems with MEMS-based electrochemical cells at low ppm concentrations is a real engineering headache that I don't think has been properly solved for long-term unattended deployment.

**What genuinely excites me:**

**Distributed acoustic sensing on existing infrastructure.** The idea that you can interrogate a fiber optic cable — potentially one that's already in a conduit for communications purposes — and use Rayleigh backscatter to get thermal and vibration profiles along the entire length of a cable run is, I think, genuinely underappreciated. The hardware is still expensive and the spatial resolution versus sensing length tradeoff is real, but the marginal cost argument of using _already-deployed fiber_ is compelling in a way that purpose-built sensor runs never will be. The question is whether the signal you get is actually useful for distinguishing failure precursors versus installation artifacts — and I think the answer is "not yet, but getting there."

**On-bushing CT-based monitoring for transformers.** There's been some clever work on extracting bushing condition information from the capacitance current you're already measuring if you have a bushing tap. The bushing is statistically one of the leading causes of transformer failures and historically requires either periodic DDF (dielectric dissipation factor) testing with the transformer offline, or expensive online monitoring equipment. The idea of getting that signal essentially for free from an already-installed measurement is the kind of thing that makes me irrationally happy. The precision requirements are gnarly — you're looking at phase angle differences in the sub-millidegree range — but the hardware to do it is becoming achievable.

**UHF PD antenna arrays for transformer monitoring.** Not new conceptually, but the combination of cheap wideband UHF front ends (driven by the SDR and 5G infrastructure boom giving us good LNAs at commodity prices), better EMI characterization tools, and actually serious ML applied to the time-difference-of-arrival localization problem — that combination feels like it's about to tip. The historical problem is that transformer substations are _incredibly_ RF-noisy environments and distinguishing internal PD from external interference requires either shielded test environments or really careful pattern recognition. I think the pattern recognition side is finally getting good enough.

**The trend I think is inevitable:**

Continuous monitoring becoming the _default_ rather than the exception for anything above, say, 15kV. The current paradigm — periodic offline testing, visual inspection, DGA sampling by a technician with a syringe — is an artifact of when continuous monitoring was prohibitively expensive. That cost curve has moved dramatically. The thing blocking adoption isn't technology anymore, it's _institutional inertia in utilities_, the lack of standardized data formats (IEC 61850 helps for protection and control but the condition monitoring side is still a mess), and frankly the absence of a trusted body of evidence that says "here's the false positive rate, here's the lead time before failure, here's the actuarial table." That evidence base is being built right now by the early adopters, and once it exists, the procurement justification writes itself.

The route we'll take is going to be messier than people expect. It'll go through a period of "we bolted on some sensors and now we have a dashboard nobody looks at" before it gets to genuine closed-loop maintenance optimization. That intermediate state frustrates me but it's probably unavoidable.

**The project I'd most want to work on:**

An integrated sensor node specifically designed for _pad-mounted distribution transformers_ — the ones in the green boxes you see in residential neighborhoods and small commercial areas. There are about 50 million distribution transformers in the US grid alone. Almost none of them have any continuous monitoring. They fail in ways that cause outages affecting real people, and the failure modes — overload-driven insulation degradation, moisture ingress, mechanical loosening of connections causing hot spots — are all detectable with combinations of acoustic, thermal, and DGA sensing if you design the sensor package intelligently.

The interesting engineering challenge is the power budget and communications problem: you want something that runs off a CT on the primary winding (so it's self-powered, no battery maintenance), operates in a sealed enclosure in extreme temperature ranges, and communicates over whatever wireless infrastructure happens to be nearby. And it has to cost _very_ little — like, tens of dollars in volume — or the economics don't work for distribution-level assets. That constraint-driven design problem, where you're trying to extract maximum diagnostic signal within brutal cost and power envelopes, is exactly the kind of thing I find genuinely beautiful to work on.

The other thing I'd want to do alongside it is build the failure mode ontology properly — like, actually catalog what the sensor signatures of incipient failure look like versus healthy aging versus benign anomalies, with rigorously labeled ground truth data from controlled failures. Because the ML people are going to show up and want to do something useful, and right now the training data situation in this industry is _atrocious_. That unglamorous data infrastructure work is what determines whether any of the clever sensing hardware actually delivers on its promise.
### Microgrid architecture for off-grid or weak-grid industrial ties
Remote mines, islands, off-grid resorts, disaster response infrastructure. The components exist (solar, storage, genset, inverters) but the _integrated system design_ — how everything talks to each other, how you size it, how you make it installable and maintainable by non-PhDs — is genuinely not solved at the small-commercial scale. Your First Mode background is almost directly applicable here.
#### Respones:
The thing that genuinely keeps me up at night is the **controls problem at the system level**. Not the component controls — those are largely solved. The inverter manufacturers have good low-level control loops. The BMS vendors know how to balance cells. The problem is the _emergent behavior_ when you strap all these independently-optimized subsystems together and ask them to agree on who's the master frequency reference right now, and what happens in the 200 milliseconds after a genset trips offline and the battery inverter hasn't quite caught up. That gap — between "the components work" and "the integrated system is stable under all credible fault sequences" — is where people get hurt or where a mine loses production for eight hours at $400k/hour.

**What's genuinely exciting me right now:**

Grid-forming inverters are the thing I find most technically beautiful at the moment. The shift from grid-following to grid-forming is underappreciated outside specialist circles. A grid-following inverter is essentially a dependent — it needs an existing voltage and frequency reference to lock onto. Fine when you have a strong grid. But in an islanded microgrid, something has to _be_ the grid. Traditionally that was the genset, which is why you sized your genset to handle motor starting transients even if your average load was a fifth of that. Grid-forming inverters can synthesize a voltage and frequency reference from nothing, handle the transient, and let you downsize or even eliminate the genset for many operating conditions. The math underlying virtual synchronous machine algorithms — where you're essentially emulating rotational inertia in software — is genuinely clever. You're solving a differential equation in the control loop to make electrons behave like they have angular momentum. That's beautiful.

The catch nobody talks about enough: two grid-forming sources in the same island that don't have a well-designed droop coordination scheme will fight each other. The interaction dynamics are subtle and can produce oscillations that look fine in steady state and absolutely fall apart under a load step. I've seen simulation results that look great and then the physical system has a 2Hz power oscillation that the engineers couldn't explain for three weeks. The physics was right, the implementation had a sign error in one droop coefficient.

**Second-life battery economics** are approaching an inflection point that I think is underappreciated. EV battery packs getting retired at 70-80% state of health because they no longer meet automotive range requirements are going to flood the market. The challenge isn't the cells — it's that every pack has a proprietary BMS, proprietary communication protocols, and was designed to be a unit, not a building block. The people who figure out how to make a standardized integration layer — essentially a BMS-of-BMSes that can talk to arbitrary pack hardware and present a consistent interface to the site controller — that's a genuinely valuable piece of IP. The cells are nearly free. The integration is the hard part.

**Liquid-cooled storage in severe environments** is more interesting than it sounds. If you're putting a BESS at a mine in the Atacama or northern Canada, thermal management stops being an afterthought. Lithium cell performance degrades significantly below about 10°C and you have serious safety considerations above 45°C. The sites that need microgrids most — remote, harsh environments — are exactly the sites where passive thermal management fails. The companies doing integrated liquid cooling with waste heat recovery (using battery thermal rejection to heat the control building, for instance) are solving a real problem elegantly.

**The trend I think is inevitable:**

Microgrids become the _default_ architecture, not the backup architecture. Right now the mental model is "grid is normal, microgrid is for when you can't have grid." I think that inverts. The reliability and resilience characteristics of a well-designed microgrid — where you know _exactly_ what your generation and storage assets are, where you're not dependent on transmission infrastructure that's increasingly stressed — will make it the preferred option for any industrial facility with serious uptime requirements. The utilities will hate this for about a decade and then figure out how to make money from it.

The route we take gets there through **islanding capability becoming standard rather than special**. Right now, most commercial solar-plus-storage installations explicitly _cannot_ island — they're required to trip offline when grid voltage deviates, which is the exact moment you'd want them most. The interconnection standards are changing, slowly, and the technical barriers are mostly solved. When islanding capability is a standard feature of every commercial inverter system, the incremental cost of true microgrid capability approaches zero.

**The problems that actually keep me up:**

Protection coordination in inverter-dominated systems. This is genuinely unsolved at the small-commercial scale and it scares me. Traditional overcurrent protection assumes fault current is roughly proportional to system capacity — a big fault puts out a lot of current, your breaker sees it, trips in milliseconds. Inverters are current-limited. A fault in an inverter-dominated microgrid might produce fault current that's only 1.5x normal operating current. Your overcurrent protection, set to avoid nuisance tripping, won't see it. You can have a sustained fault condition that doesn't trip protection and turns into a fire. The solutions — directional protection, differential protection, communication-assisted tripping — all exist at utility scale and cost utility-scale money. Translating that to a 500kW island that has to be maintained by an electrician who's not a protection engineer is not done.

The **maintainability problem** is deeply underappreciated. You can design a beautiful system that a controls engineer can troubleshoot. That system goes to a remote mine. The controls engineer flies home. Eighteen months later something is degraded — not failed, _degraded_ — and the person on site is an electrician who is very good at what electricians do and has no idea why the site controller is showing a weird state-of-charge discrepancy. The design question that I think is most important and least addressed: how do you build a system where the failure modes are _diagnosable_ by a non-specialist, where the system itself is helping you understand what's wrong with it? Not just alarms, but actual fault isolation and guided remediation.

**Projects I'd want to work on:**

A proper reference architecture — not a product, an _architecture_ — for the 100kW to 2MW islanded industrial site. Specifying not just the components but the communication topology, the protection philosophy, the control hierarchy, the commissioning procedure, the diagnostic framework. Something that a competent engineer could follow and get a system that behaves predictably. The industry has product data sheets. It doesn't have this.

And honestly? The disaster response infrastructure problem. When a hurricane takes out grid infrastructure for three weeks, the response is diesel generators that run out of fuel, have to be manually tended, and have a meantime-between-failures that's... not great under sustained heavy use. A rapidly-deployable microgrid — containerized, pre-engineered, designed to accept whatever renewable generation is available on site — that can be stood up by people who are not power systems engineers, in a situation where everything is chaotic... that's technically hard and the stakes are as high as it gets. The system has to be _robust to the installer not being an expert_ in a way that almost no power system currently is.
