## Hypothesis 2: The ‘battery in everything’ model works

That it makes sense from a climate perspective and is financially viable (logistics, production, makes money) to put little batteries in everything. Can we make it ikea-good

### Advantages:

- Widely distributed power - balances the grid
- Potentially really cool sensor data - that seems like the sort of thing you sell to the government and try not to feel too awful about when you go to sleep and hope no one really notices and nothing bad really happens and it just sort of goes away, the privacy concerns

###  Key questions:

- Would all of these have to be coordinated and thus talking to one another? Is there a passive way to do this, i.e. even if can get more optimization from active, if can safely default to a passive way of seeing who is drawing how much power, with no measurement at the device and thus regulation of the device’s power draw
- Do we need any government approvals for power use data etc for this? It’s just measuring the power draw at the device, so maybe also amalgamates into grid data
- Can we predict outages? How much would utilities pay to prevent outages?

### Disadvantages:

- At a system-wide level lots of tiny batteries is a materially inefficient way of storing energy and we already have so many material issues in our world - but probably want a mix of different sized batteries
